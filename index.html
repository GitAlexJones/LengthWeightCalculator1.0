🔍 Crest Height → Flow Rate (GPM)
Measure the crest height in your sump and look up the flow rate using the hatchery reference chart

The crest height is the height of water flowing over the top edge (crest) of a sump pipe. It tells you how much water is flowing.

  1. Find the sump. It's the vertical pipe where water overflows from your raceway or tank.
  2. Measure the diameter of the sump pipe (6", 8", 10", or 12").
  3. Measure the crest height — the distance from the top rim of the sump down to where the water surface is inside the pipe. Use a ruler held across the top.
  4. Measure in multiple spots around the rim and take the average (the water may be uneven).
  5. Look up the flow on the chart below using your sump diameter and crest height.
💡
Pro Tip: Measure crest height in multiple locations around the sump and take the average. Water height can vary!

⏱️ Exchange Time
How long does it take to replace all the water in your tank, pond, or raceway?

Exchange Time tells you how many minutes it takes for the full volume of water to be replaced by new incoming water. This is critical for fish health — it determines how quickly waste is flushed and fresh oxygen arrives.

Exchange Time = Volume ÷ Flow Rate

Keep units the same! If flow is in GPM (gallons per minute), make sure volume is in gallons.

⚠️
Common mistake: Mixing units! If your volume is in cubic feet, convert to gallons first (1 ft³ = 7.48 gallons).

📚 Learn the Theory
Understanding the science behind every hatchery flow calculation

Where Does All This Math Come From?

Every flow rate, volume, and exchange calculation you use at the hatchery traces back to one fundamental principle in physics:

🎓
The Continuity Equation — based on the Conservation of Mass. Water flowing into a system must equal water flowing out (it can't just appear or disappear). This was established by scientists Leonhard Euler and Daniel Bernoulli in the 1700s.

In simple terms: What goes in must come out. If you know the pipe size and the water speed, you can calculate exactly how much water is flowing. If you know the volume and the flow, you can calculate how long it takes to replace the water.

General Measuring Guidelines

From your class lesson — these habits will prevent the most common errors:

✅
Record units with ALL measurements — say "25 cm" not just "25"
Measure diameter first for circles → divide by 2 for radius
Convert units BEFORE plugging into formulas
Round at the final step, not each step — small errors compound quickly!
Measure twice — check with a partner when possible
🚫
Common Errors to Avoid:
• Start at the 0 mark, not the edge of the tool
• Keep rulers and tapes straight — no bending or sagging
• Read liquid volume at eye level from the bottom of the meniscus
• Measure straight through the center for diameters
• Be mindful of interior vs exterior dimensions

The Key Formulas

1. Surface Area

Rectangle

A = L × W

L = length, W = width. Used for flat surfaces of tanks and raceways.

Circle

A = π × r²

r = radius (half of diameter), π ≈ 3.14159. Used for round tanks and pipe openings.

2. Volume

Rectangle / Raceway / Trough

V = L × W × H

L = length, W = width, H = height (depth). Think of it as stacking layers of area.

Cylinder (Round Tank, Pipe, Bucket)

V = π × r² × h

r = radius, h = height. It's just the circle area multiplied by height.

Cone (Conical Tanks, Funnels)

V = ⅓ × π × r² × h

Same as cylinder but divided by 3 — a cone fits exactly 3 times inside a cylinder.

⭐ Cone-Bottom Pond (Pond 2 from Class)

The floor gently slopes to a cone-shaped bottom. You split it into a cylinder on top + cone on the bottom:

h₃ = h₁ − h₂ (sump depth − wall depth = cone height) V = (π × r² × h₂) + (⅓ × π × r² × h₃)

Measure the full diameter including under any suspended walkway.

3. Flow Rate & Exchange Time

Exchange Time

Exchange Time = Volume ÷ Flow Rate

If volume is in gallons and flow in GPM, the result is in minutes. This tells you how long it takes to completely replace the water in your system.

Measuring Flow with Crest Height

In hatcheries, we measure the crest height — the height of water flowing over the top of a sump pipe. Using the sump diameter and crest height, we look up the flow rate (GPM) on a reference chart. This method comes from weir flow principles where discharge is proportional to the head of water raised to the 3/2 power.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Raceway Volume

Problem: A raceway is 100 ft long, 5 ft wide, and 2.5 ft deep. How many gallons does it hold?

V = L × W × H = 100 × 5 × 2.5 = 1,250 ft³ Gallons = 1,250 × 7.48 = 9,350 gallons

Answer: The raceway holds about 9,350 gallons.

Example 2 — Exchange Time

Problem: That same 9,350-gallon raceway has a flow of 113 GPM (from a 12" sump with 1" crest height). What's the exchange time?

Exchange Time = 9,350 ÷ 113 = 82.7 minutes ≈ 1.4 hours

Answer: The water is fully replaced about every 83 minutes. That's roughly 0.7 exchanges per hour.

Example 3 — Round Tank Volume

Problem: A circular rearing tank is 12 ft in diameter and 4 ft deep. How many gallons?

r = 12 ÷ 2 = 6 ft V = π × 6² × 4 = 3.14159 × 36 × 4 = 452.4 ft³ Gallons = 452.4 × 7.48 = 3,384 gallons

Answer: About 3,384 gallons.

Example 4 — Cone-Bottom Pond (Pond 2)

Problem: Pond 2 is 20 ft diameter. The sump depth (h₁) is 6 ft and the wall depth (h₂) is 3 ft. What's the volume?

r = 20 ÷ 2 = 10 ft h₃ = h₁ − h₂ = 6 − 3 = 3 ft (cone height) Cylinder: π × 10² × 3 = 942.5 ft³ Cone: ⅓ × π × 10² × 3 = 314.2 ft³ Total = 942.5 + 314.2 = 1,256.6 ft³ = 9,400 gallons

Answer: Pond 2 holds about 9,400 gallons.

Example 5 — Using the Crest Chart

Problem: You measure a 10" sump with crest heights of 1.5", 1.25", and 1.5" at three spots. What's the flow?

Average crest = (1.5 + 1.25 + 1.5) ÷ 3 = 1.42"

Round to the nearest value on the chart: 1.375" or 1.5"

At 1.5" on a 10" sump → 176 GPM At 1.375" on a 10" sump → 153 GPM

Answer: The flow is approximately 153–176 GPM. You could interpolate to about 165 GPM.

Why This Matters for Fish

In a hatchery, water flow is literally life or death for the fish. Here's why each calculation matters:

🫧
Oxygen: Fish need dissolved oxygen. Fresh flowing water brings oxygen in. If the exchange time is too long, oxygen drops and fish suffocate.
⚗️
Ammonia: Fish produce ammonia as waste. Flowing water flushes it out. If flow is too slow, ammonia builds up and becomes toxic.
🌡️
Temperature: Incoming water helps regulate temperature. Consistent flow = consistent temperature = less stress on the fish.
🧹
Waste Removal: Uneaten feed and feces need to be flushed out. Adequate flow keeps the rearing environment clean.

For salmonid hatcheries (trout, salmon), a water exchange rate of 2–3 times per hour is common. That means the entire tank volume should be replaced every 20–30 minutes.
